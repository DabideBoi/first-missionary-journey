Paul's First Missionary Journey

An Exhaustive Study

Acts 13:1 - 14:28

"For so the Lord has commanded us: 'I have set you as a light to the Gentiles, that you should be for salvation to the ends of the earth.'" (Acts 13:47)

I. Background and Context

Paul the Apostle

  • Formerly Saul of Tarsus, a devout Pharisee (Philippians 3:5)
  • Persecuted Christians before his conversion (Acts 8:1-3; 9:1-2; Galatians 1:13)
  • Encountered the risen Christ on the Damascus Road (Acts 9:3-9)
  • Called specifically to the Gentile mission: "He is a chosen vessel of Mine to bear My name before Gentiles, kings, and the children of Israel" (Acts 9:15)
  • Spent formative years in Arabia and Damascus before ministry (Galatians 1:17-18)

The Church at Antioch

  • Major hub for Christian ministry after persecution in Jerusalem (Acts 11:19-21)
  • First place believers were called "Christians" (Acts 11:26)
  • Multi-ethnic leadership: "Barnabas, Simeon called Niger, Lucius of Cyrene, Manaen who had been brought up with Herod the tetrarch, and Saul" (Acts 13:1)
  • Demonstrated practical love by sending relief to Judean believers during famine (Acts 11:27-30)
  • Located in Syria, Antioch was the third-largest city in the Roman Empire after Rome and Alexandria, with an estimated population of 500,000

II. Divine Commissioning

"Now in the church that was at Antioch there were certain prophets and teachers... As they ministered to the Lord and fasted, the Holy Spirit said, 'Now separate to Me Barnabas and Saul for the work to which I have called them.'"

Acts 13:1-2

  • The Holy Spirit spoke while believers in Antioch were "ministering to the Lord" (λειτουργούντων) and fasting—a term suggesting formal worship
  • "Then, having fasted and prayed, and laid hands on them, they sent them away" (Acts 13:3)—following ancient Jewish commissioning customs
  • Commissioned for missionary service (approx. 46-48 A.D.)
  • This marks a pivotal shift in Acts from Peter's ministry to Paul's, and from Jerusalem to the wider Gentile world
  • The Greek word for "sent" (ἀπέλυσαν) in Acts 13:3 is the root of our word "apostle"—one who is sent

III. Journey Overview

Paul's First Missionary Journey Route

  1. Antioch (Syria) - Departure point (Acts 13:1-3)
  2. Seleucia - Port city, 16 miles from Antioch (Acts 13:4)
  3. Salamis (Cyprus) - "They preached the word of God in the synagogues of the Jews" (Acts 13:5)
  4. Paphos (Cyprus) - Encounter with Bar-Jesus (Elymas), conversion of Sergius Paulus (Acts 13:6-12)
  5. Perga (Pamphylia) - John Mark departs to Jerusalem (Acts 13:13)
  6. Antioch in Pisidia - First recorded sermon, mixed response (Acts 13:14-52)
  7. Iconium - "A great multitude both of the Jews and of the Greeks believed" (Acts 14:1-5)
  8. Lystra - Healing miracle, mistaken for gods, Paul stoned (Acts 14:6-20)
  9. Derbe - "They preached the gospel to that city and made many disciples" (Acts 14:20-21)
  10. Return trip - Retraced steps, "appointed elders in every church" (Acts 14:21-25)
  11. Attalia - Coastal city, set sail back (Acts 14:25-26)
  12. Antioch (Syria) - "Reported all that God had done with them" (Acts 14:26-28)

IV. Key Events: Cyprus

  • "When they arrived at Salamis, they preached the word of God in the synagogues of the Jews" (Acts 13:5)
  • At Paphos, met Sergius Paulus, Roman proconsul—described as "an intelligent man" (Acts 13:7)
  • Archaeological evidence: Inscriptions bearing the name "Sergius Paulus" have been discovered on Cyprus, confirming Luke's historical accuracy
  • Conflict with Bar-Jesus/Elymas the sorcerer:
    • "O full of all deceit and all fraud, you son of the devil, you enemy of all righteousness, will you not cease perverting the straight ways of the Lord?" (Acts 13:10)
    • "And now, indeed, the hand of the Lord is upon you, and you shall be blind, not seeing the sun for a time" (Acts 13:11)
  • Sergius Paulus believed, "astonished at the teaching of the Lord" (Acts 13:12)—first recorded high-ranking Roman official convert

Significance:

Demonstrates spiritual warfare and Paul's emergence as team leader (shift from "Barnabas and Saul" in Acts 13:7 to "Paul and his party" in Acts 13:13). Cyprus was strategically important as Barnabas's homeland (Acts 4:36), providing a natural starting point with established connections.

IV. Key Events: Perga & Antioch in Pisidia

Perga in Pamphylia

  • "Now when Paul and his party set sail from Paphos, they came to Perga in Pamphylia; and John, departing from them, returned to Jerusalem" (Acts 13:13)
  • His early departure later causes a sharp dispute between Paul and Barnabas: "Barnabas was determined to take with them John called Mark... But Paul insisted that they should not take with them the one who had departed from them in Pamphylia" (Acts 15:37-38)
  • Historical context: Perga was in a malaria-infested region, and the journey ahead involved crossing the dangerous Taurus Mountains—possibly contributing factors to Mark's departure
  • Later reconciliation: Mark eventually regained Paul's trust, becoming "useful to me for ministry" (2 Timothy 4:11)

Antioch in Pisidia

  • Paul delivers his first recorded sermon in Acts (13:16-41)
  • Structure: Old Testament history (13:17-25), Jesus as fulfillment (13:26-37), justification through Christ (13:38-41)
  • Quotes four Old Testament passages: Psalm 2:7, Isaiah 55:3, Psalm 16:10, and Habakkuk 1:5
  • Some Jews accept, others become jealous and stir persecution: "But when the Jews saw the multitudes, they were filled with envy" (Acts 13:45)
  • Paul and Barnabas boldly declare: "It was necessary that the word of God should be spoken to you first; but since you reject it... we turn to the Gentiles" (Acts 13:46)
  • Gentiles rejoice: "I have set you as a light to the Gentiles, that you should be for salvation to the ends of the earth" (Isaiah 49:6, quoted in Acts 13:47)
  • Cultural context: The synagogue service followed a pattern of prayers, Scripture readings, and exposition—Paul utilized this familiar structure

Key Theological Insight: Justification by faith - "By Him everyone who believes is justified from all things from which you could not be justified by the law of Moses" (Acts 13:39)

IV. Key Events: Iconium & Lystra

Iconium

  • Spoke in the synagogue; "a great multitude both of the Jews and of the Greeks believed" (Acts 14:1)
  • "But the unbelieving Jews stirred up the Gentiles and poisoned their minds against the brethren" (Acts 14:2)
  • "Therefore they stayed there a long time, speaking boldly in the Lord" (Acts 14:3)
  • Paul and Barnabas perform miraculous signs and wonders: "the Lord... was bearing witness to the word of His grace, granting signs and wonders to be done by their hands" (Acts 14:3)
  • "But the multitude of the city was divided: part sided with the Jews, and part with the apostles" (Acts 14:4)
  • Attempted stoning, so "they became aware of it and fled to Lystra and Derbe" (Acts 14:6)
  • Historical note: Iconium (modern Konya, Turkey) was a major Phrygian city at the intersection of important trade routes

Lystra

  • Paul heals a man crippled from birth who "had never walked" (Acts 14:8-10)
  • Locals believe Paul is Hermes and Barnabas is Zeus: "The gods have come down to us in the likeness of men!" (Acts 14:11)
  • Cultural context: Local legend (recorded by Ovid) told of Zeus and Hermes visiting the region in human form and being rejected by all except one elderly couple
  • "The priest of Zeus, whose temple was in front of their city, brought oxen and garlands to the gates, intending to sacrifice with the multitudes" (Acts 14:13)
  • Paul and Barnabas object: "Men, why are you doing these things? We also are men with the same nature as you" (Acts 14:15)
  • First recorded sermon to pagans focuses on creation and natural revelation rather than Jewish history
  • Dramatic shift from adoration to stoning Paul: "Jews from Antioch and Iconium came there; and having persuaded the multitudes, they stoned Paul and dragged him out of the city, supposing him to be dead" (Acts 14:19)
  • Paul miraculously recovers: "However, when the disciples gathered around him, he rose up and went into the city" (Acts 14:20)
  • Timothy's hometown: Paul likely met Timothy's family here (Acts 16:1-3)

First sermon to a purely pagan audience: "Turn from these useless things to the living God, who made the heaven, the earth, the sea, and all things that are in them" (Acts 14:15)

IV. Key Events: Derbe & Return Journey

Derbe

  • "And the next day he departed with Barnabas to Derbe" (Acts 14:20)
  • Made many disciples: "And when they had preached the gospel to that city and made many disciples" (Acts 14:21)
  • No specific opposition recorded—a peaceful ministry after intense persecution
  • Gaius, a later companion of Paul, was from Derbe (Acts 20:4)
  • Archaeological note: Derbe was the easternmost point of their journey, located in the Roman province of Galatia
  • Considered a successful and tranquil segment of the journey

Return Journey

  • Retraced steps: "They returned to Lystra, Iconium, and Antioch" (Acts 14:21)
  • "Strengthening the souls of the disciples, exhorting them to continue in the faith, and saying, 'We must through many tribulations enter the kingdom of God'" (Acts 14:22)
  • Appointed elders: "So when they had appointed elders in every church, and prayed with fasting, they commended them to the Lord in whom they had believed" (Acts 14:23)
  • Cultural context: The Greek term for "appointed" (χειροτονήσαντες) literally means "to stretch out the hand" and was used for voting by show of hands—suggesting possible congregational involvement
  • "After they had passed through Pisidia, they came to Pamphylia. Now when they had preached the word in Perga, they went down to Attalia" (Acts 14:24-25)
  • Final return to Antioch (Syria): "From there they sailed to Antioch, where they had been commended to the grace of God for the work which they had completed" (Acts 14:26)
  • Reported back: "Now when they had come and gathered the church together, they reported all that God had done with them, and that He had opened the door of faith to the Gentiles" (Acts 14:27)
  • "So they stayed there a long time with the disciples" (Acts 14:28)

V. Key Participants

Name Role
Paul Lead missionary, preacher, theologian; described himself as "apostle to the Gentiles" (Romans 11:13)
Barnabas Paul's companion, encourager ("Son of Encouragement," Acts 4:36), co-evangelist; Levite from Cyprus who sold land to support the early church (Acts 4:36-37)
John Mark Assistant/helper who departed early (Acts 13:13); cousin of Barnabas (Colossians 4:10); later wrote the Gospel of Mark and became "useful" to Paul (2 Timothy 4:11)
Sergius Paulus Roman proconsul of Cyprus and convert; described as "an intelligent man" (Acts 13:7); archaeological evidence confirms his historical existence
Bar-Jesus/Elymas Jewish sorcerer and false prophet (Acts 13:6); opponent of the Gospel, struck blind; name "Elymas" means "magician" in Arabic
Jewish leaders Mixed response — some believed (Acts 13:43), others were "filled with envy" (Acts 13:45) and "stirred up the devout and prominent women and the chief men of the city" (Acts 13:50)
Gentiles Many open to the Gospel — "when the Gentiles heard this, they were glad and glorified the word of the Lord" (Acts 13:48); fulfillment of Isaiah's prophecy of light to the Gentiles (Acts 13:47)

VI. Theological Significance

Expansion to Gentiles

First major effort to purposefully evangelize non-Jewish regions, fulfilling Jesus's commission: "You shall be witnesses to Me in Jerusalem, and in all Judea and Samaria, and to the end of the earth" (Acts 1:8)

Apostolic Authority

Paul proclaims the Gospel with boldness amidst persecution, authenticates apostleship with "signs, wonders, and mighty deeds" (2 Corinthians 12:12), fulfilling Jesus's promise that "these signs will follow those who believe" (Mark 16:17)

Justification by Faith

Emphasizes faith over the Law for salvation: "By Him everyone who believes is justified from all things from which you could not be justified by the law of Moses" (Acts 13:38-39), anticipating Paul's fuller development in Romans and Galatians

Persecution as Normative

The journey is marked by opposition and stoning, but also fruitfulness, confirming Jesus's warning: "In the world you will have tribulation; but be of good cheer, I have overcome the world" (John 16:33) and Paul's later teaching that "all who desire to live godly in Christ Jesus will suffer persecution" (2 Timothy 3:12)

VII. Lessons and Impact

For the Early Church

  • Shift from primarily Jewish to multi-ethnic church, fulfilling God's promise to Abraham that "in you all the families of the earth shall be blessed" (Genesis 12:3)
  • Leadership multiplication through elder appointment, establishing the pattern described in Titus 1:5: "appoint elders in every city"
  • Encouraged other missionary efforts, setting a precedent for the Great Commission (Matthew 28:19-20)
  • Demonstrated the Holy Spirit's active guidance in mission work: "So, being sent out by the Holy Spirit, they went down to Seleucia" (Acts 13:4)

For Believers Today

  • Availability of salvation to all nations: "There is neither Jew nor Greek, there is neither slave nor free, there is neither male nor female; for you are all one in Christ Jesus" (Galatians 3:28)
  • Importance of courage, discernment, and faithfulness in mission: "Be strong in the Lord and in the power of His might" (Ephesians 6:10)
  • Model of church planting, discipleship, and leadership development: "The things that you have heard from me among many witnesses, commit these to faithful men who will be able to teach others also" (2 Timothy 2:2)
  • Willingness to suffer for Gospel advancement: "For to you it has been granted on behalf of Christ, not only to believe in Him, but also to suffer for His sake" (Philippians 1:29)

VIII. Timeline

~46 A.D.

Departure from Antioch

"As they ministered to the Lord and fasted, the Holy Spirit said, 'Now separate to Me Barnabas and Saul for the work to which I have called them'" (Acts 13:2)

46-48 A.D.

Ministry in Cyprus and Asia Minor

Preaching, miracles, church planting, persecution; historical context: This occurred during the reign of Emperor Claudius (41-54 A.D.), a relatively stable period in the Roman Empire that facilitated travel and communication

~48 A.D.

Return to Antioch

"They reported all that God had done with them, and that He had opened the door of faith to the Gentiles" (Acts 14:27); this report likely influenced the Jerusalem Council's decisions (Acts 15) regarding Gentile believers

Conclusion

Paul's First Missionary Journey marked a groundbreaking phase in Christian history — the Gospel broke beyond Jewish boundaries and took root among the Gentiles, fulfilling Jesus's words that "this gospel of the kingdom will be preached in all the world as a witness to all the nations" (Matthew 24:14).

With Spirit-led boldness, signs and wonders, sound doctrine, and pastoral care, Paul and Barnabas laid the foundation for the global mission of the Church, demonstrating that "the word of God grew and multiplied" (Acts 12:24) despite opposition.

"They gathered the church together and reported all that God had done through them and how He had opened a door of faith to the Gentiles."

Acts 14:27
